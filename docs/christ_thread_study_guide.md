# Study Guide: The Christ Thread Through the 66 Books

This is a different kind of guide than `docs/study_guide.md`. That one asks an empirical
question — *does an author's style survive translation?* — and answers it with statistics.
This one asks a theological question the rest of this project doesn't touch: **if Scripture
has one ultimate Author working through many human ones (2 Timothy 3:16, 2 Peter 1:21), what
is he saying, and where does it point?**

Tradition's answer, stated plainly in Luke 24:27 and 44–45, is that it all points to Jesus —
even the parts written centuries before he lived. This guide is a reading path to test that
claim for yourself, the same way the rest of the project tests its own claims: not by taking
someone's word for it, but by going to the text.

Interesting side note, given what the rest of this repo found: the stylometry shows these are
*genuinely different human voices* — Isaiah doesn't write like Paul, Paul doesn't write like
the author of Hebrews, John doesn't write like Revelation's author. Real personalities, real
idiolects, statistically distinguishable. The theological claim isn't that God flattened those
voices into one — it's that he spoke *through* all of them toward one message. Diversity of
style and unity of message aren't in tension; the first is actually evidence for the second
being deliberate rather than coincidental.

Use `src/reader.py` / `notebooks/parallel_reader.ipynb` for any verse below you want to see in
the original Hebrew or Greek, with transliteration and gloss next to the KJV (and ESV, if you
set `ESV_API_KEY`).

---

## 1. The promise, planted early (Genesis)

**Genesis 3:15** — right after the fall, God tells the serpent: "it shall bruise thy head, and
thou shalt bruise his heel." Read in context (Genesis 3:1–15). This is traditionally called the
*protoevangelium* — the first gospel — the first hint, in the Bible's own opening chapters,
that someone is coming who will undo what just went wrong, at a cost to himself ("bruise his
heel").

**Genesis 22:1–18** — Abraham told to sacrifice his only son Isaac on a mountain; a substitute
is provided at the last moment ("God will provide himself a lamb," v. 8). Read the whole scene
slowly — the substitution, the mountain, the language of "only son" (v. 2, 12, 16). Compare
later against John 3:16 and Romans 8:32, which reuse almost identical phrasing.

**Question to sit with:** why would a book of law and genealogy open with a promise like this
at all, this early, before anything else has been established?

---

## 2. A lamb that stands in your place (Exodus)

**Exodus 12:1–13, 21–23** — the Passover instructions: a lamb without blemish, killed, its
blood marking the doorframe, so judgment passes over. Read it as instructions given to real
people escaping real slavery, not yet as symbol — then read 1 Corinthians 5:7 ("Christ our
passover is sacrificed for us") and John 1:29 ("Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the
sin of the world") to see how the New Testament writers read it back.

**Question to sit with:** what does it mean that the marker of safety wasn't good behavior, but
blood on a specific place, applied by faith that the instruction was trustworthy?

---

## 3. A king and a priest who don't fit the categories (Genesis, Psalms)

**Genesis 14:18–20** — Melchizedek, king of Salem and "priest of the most high God," blesses
Abram — with no genealogy given, no beginning or end recorded, in a system where priesthood was
otherwise strictly hereditary (Levi's line).

**Psalm 110** — a psalm of David that describes someone David calls "my Lord" (v. 1) sitting at
God's right hand, and declares him "a priest for ever after the order of Melchizedek" (v. 4).
David, the king, calling someone else his Lord is the puzzle Jesus later poses directly to the
religious leaders in Matthew 22:41–46 — read that exchange right after this psalm.

**Question to sit with:** why introduce a priesthood-outside-the-priesthood this early, and then
have Israel's greatest king defer to someone even higher?

---

## 4. Named centuries ahead of time (the Prophets)

Read these roughly in this order — each adds a different, specific detail:

- **Micah 5:2** — born in Bethlehem, a ruler "whose goings forth have been from of old, from
  everlasting."
- **Isaiah 7:14** — born of a virgin, called Immanuel, "God with us."
- **Isaiah 9:6–7** — a child born who is also called "The mighty God, The everlasting Father,"
  reigning on David's throne forever.
- **Isaiah 53** (the whole chapter, slowly) — "despised and rejected of men," "wounded for our
  transgressions," "the chastisement of our peace was upon him," "he was cut off out of the land
  of the living," a death "with the wicked," yet "he shall see his seed... he shall prolong his
  days." Suffering and vindication in the same figure.
- **Zechariah 9:9** — a king entering "lowly, and riding upon an ass" (compare Matthew 21:1–11).
- **Zechariah 12:10** — "they shall look upon me whom they have pierced" — spoken in the voice of
  God himself (compare John 19:34–37).
- **Psalm 22** — read it as a whole poem, not just isolated verses: opens "My God, my God, why
  hast thou forsaken me?" (compare Matthew 27:46), and includes "they pierced my hands and my
  feet" (v. 16) and casting lots for a garment (v. 18) — written centuries before crucifixion
  existed as a Roman practice.

**Question to sit with:** these come from different authors, different centuries, different
genres (prophecy, psalm) — this project's own stylometry would treat Isaiah and the Psalter as
about as stylistically unrelated as texts get. Does the *content* converge anyway?

## 5. What he said about himself (the Gospels)

The prophets describe someone in advance. The Gospels record what he said about *himself* —
worth separating from what others said about him.

- **John's seven "I am" statements**, each an ordinary image loaded with claim: bread of life
  (6:35), light of the world (8:12), the door (10:9), the good shepherd (10:11), the
  resurrection and the life (11:25), the way, the truth, and the life (14:6), the true vine
  (15:1). Read John 8:58 alongside Exodus 3:14 — "before Abraham was, I am" directly echoes the
  divine name given to Moses; the reaction in the next verse (they pick up stones) shows his
  hearers caught the claim immediately.
- **Matthew 5:21–22, 27–28, 31–32, 38–39, 43–44** — "Ye have heard that it was said... but I say
  unto you." He is not citing a rabbi. He's placing his own word alongside, and correcting, the
  Law itself.
- **Mark 2:1–12** — he forgives a paralyzed man's sins before healing him, and directly answers
  the scribes' unspoken objection ("who can forgive sins but God only?") by healing as proof of
  the claim.
- **John 5:39** — his own verdict on the Hebrew Scriptures, spoken to people who knew them by
  heart: "they are they which testify of me."

**Question to sit with:** strip away everyone else's opinion of him for a moment — based only on
what he says about himself in these passages, what is he actually claiming to be?

## 6. What the early church concluded (the Epistles)

Not narrative, but reasoned, often poetic exposition — the church working out the implications.

- **Philippians 2:5–11** — widely thought to be an early Christian hymn Paul is quoting: he
  "thought it not robbery to be equal with God," yet "made himself of no reputation," took "the
  form of a servant," was "obedient unto death, even the death of the cross" — then exalted, so
  that "every knee should bow."
- **Colossians 1:15–20** — "the image of the invisible God... by him were all things created...
  and by him all things consist." Creator, not created.
- **Hebrews 1:1–4** — the verse this whole project's title question grows out of: "God... hath in
  these last days spoken unto us by his Son... who being the brightness of his glory, and the
  express image of his person." Compare this dense, formal Greek opening (see
  `docs/study_guide.md` §3 on Hebrews' style) against Philippians' hymn-like rhythm — two very
  different authorial voices reaching the same claim.
- **1 John 1:1–3** — written by someone insisting on firsthand, physical testimony: "which we
  have heard, which we have seen with our eyes, which we have looked upon, and our hands have
  handled." Not abstract theology — eyewitness claim.

**Question to sit with:** these four passages come from at least three, probably four different
authors this project's own stylometry treats as stylistically distinct people (Paul, the author
of Hebrews, John). Read side by side, how much theological daylight is actually between them?

---

## 7. Where it ends up (Revelation)

**Revelation 5:1–14** — a scroll no one is found worthy to open, until "the Lion of the tribe of
Juda" appears — and turns out to be "a Lamb as it had been slain" (v. 6). Power described as a
lion, revealed as a slaughtered lamb — the whole tension of suffering-and-triumph from Isaiah 53
and Psalm 22, resolved.

**Revelation 21:1–4, 22:1–5** — the ending image: no more death, no more tears, and "they shall
see his face." The Bible's opening scene (Genesis 1–3) is a garden with God walking among
people, lost through a fall; its closing scene is a city with God dwelling among people,
restored. Read Genesis 3 and Revelation 21–22 back to back — the shape is deliberate.

**Question to sit with:** the very last chapters resolve the very first ones. Does that feel like
66 unrelated books that happened to end up bound together, or one story told in movements?

## For your own discovery

This guide picks ~20 stops out of hundreds of candidate passages (Genesis 49:10, Numbers 24:17,
Deuteronomy 18:15, Ruth's whole book as a picture of a kinsman-redeemer, Jonah 1:17 vs. Matthew
12:40, Daniel 7:13–14, and many more are left for you). A few ways to keep going on your own,
using tools already in this repo:

- **Read chronologically through one Testament** and log every place you notice a promise,
  pattern, or title later picked up — build your own cross-reference list rather than relying on
  someone else's.
- **Use `notebooks/parallel_reader.ipynb`** on any Old Testament passage above to see the
  original Hebrew word choice up close — e.g. what exactly Isaiah 53 says under "stripes" or
  "chastisement" (this pairs directly with `docs/study_guide.md` §1's note on those same Hebrew
  roots).
- **Ask of every passage the two questions this whole project structures itself around**: what
  does the text actually say (not what I assume it says), and how does it read against the rest
  of the canon it sits inside — the same discipline this repo applies to authorship, applied here
  to meaning.

This document is deliberately outside the project's empirical/statistical scope (see README's
"Non-goals") — it's a reading path, not a testable claim, and isn't referenced from the README's
results tables for that reason.
