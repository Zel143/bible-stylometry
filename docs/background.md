# Biblical Authorship and Stylometric Consistency: Traditional Attribution vs. Critical Scholarship

## TL;DR
- **Stylometric and linguistic evidence broadly corroborates the mainstream critical picture but is weaker and more ambiguous than often claimed:** it strongly supports multiple "voices" in Isaiah and clear stylistic separation of Hebrews, Revelation, and 2 Peter from the works they are traditionally grouped with, but recent computational studies have partially undermined the once-confident case against the disputed Pauline and Pastoral letters.
- **The single most consistent finding across a century of studies** is that a core cluster of texts always groups tightly together — the seven "undisputed" Pauline letters, and the Luke–Acts pair — while traditionally-grouped outliers (Isaiah 40–66, the Pastorals, Ephesians/Colossians, Revelation vs. the Fourth Gospel, 2 Peter vs. 1 Peter) repeatedly separate out.
- **Method matters enormously:** short text length, genre and topic differences, use of secretaries (amanuenses), and the choice of statistical model can flip results, so stylometry rarely "proves" authorship on its own and is best read alongside historical and theological evidence.

## Key Findings

1. **The Pentateuch:** Tradition ascribes all five books to Moses; critical scholarship since Wellhausen posits multiple sources (J, E, D, P). Computational studies split: Radday & Shore (1985) concluded Genesis is a stylistic *unity*, but found the real divisions run along speech/narration lines, not J/E/P. More recent unsupervised machine-learning studies (Koppel et al. 2011; Yoffe et al. 2023, 2025) can separate "P" from "non-P" material at ~73–90% agreement with scholarly divisions — but they fix the number of clusters in advance and partly track genre, so they do not prove a specific number of authors.

2. **Isaiah:** The strongest OT stylometric case for multiple authorship. Radday's computerized tests (1970/1973) found statistically significant differences (e.g., word/syllable length) between chapters 1–39 and 40–66, supporting the Deutero/Trito-Isaiah consensus, though he cautiously argued style differences alone are not decisively "author-specifying."

3. **Pauline corpus:** Seven letters (Romans, 1–2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, Philemon) form a stable stylistic core. The Pastorals (1–2 Timothy, Titus), Ephesians/Colossians, and 2 Thessalonians repeatedly separate in older studies (Harrison 1921, Morton, Mealand, Savoy 2019). But Anthony Kenny (1986) found no reason to reject single authorship of twelve of the thirteen, and two 2025 studies (Pracht & McCauley) found the Pastorals do *not* differ significantly — showing the case is genuinely contested on stylometric grounds.

4. **Johannine literature and Hebrews:** The Greek of Revelation differs so markedly from the Gospel/Epistles of John that observers from Dionysius of Alexandria (3rd c.) to modern scholars doubt common authorship. Hebrews' polished Greek and rhetorical structure were recognized as un-Pauline already by Origen and Clement of Alexandria.

5. **Petrine letters:** 1 Peter and 2 Peter differ sharply in Greek quality and vocabulary; most critical scholars conclude they cannot share an author.

## Details

### Old Testament

**Pentateuch / Torah (Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy).** Traditional attribution: Moses. Critical view: the Documentary Hypothesis (Graf–Wellhausen), which sees four main sources — J (Yahwist, uses YHWH, ~9th c. BCE), [Help With Bible Study](http://helpmewithbiblestudy.org/5system_moses/notes/dh6.aspx) E (Elohist), D (Deuteronomist, mostly Deuteronomy), and P (Priestly, formulaic, genealogical, cultic) — combined by later redactors. The hypothesis explicitly arose partly from *stylistic* observations: Jean Astruc (1753) noticed alternating divine names and stylistic differences in Genesis. [CARM](https://carm.org/the-bible/the-documentary-hypothesis-of-the-pentateuch-also-known-as-the-jedp-theory/) Classical Documentarians believed the sources could be separated on stylistic criteria.

Stylometric evidence here is genuinely double-edged:
- **Radday & Shore, *Genesis: An Authorship Study in Computer-Assisted Statistical Linguistics* (Analecta Biblica 103, 1985):** A five-year study of Genesis's ~20,000 words concluded the book is a linguistic *unity*. Radday (of the Technion), reported in the Jerusalem Post/UPI (Oct. 28, 1981), put it strikingly: "The probability of Genesis having been written by one author is enormously high — 82 percent statistically"; the team stated the "82 percent identity between the J and E definitions of Genesis make their unity very highly probable." The published monograph argued there is "massive evidence" that the postulated triplicity of J/E/P "is actually a unity." [Bible Archaeology](https://biblearchaeology.org/research-articles/the-documentary-hypothesis/) Crucially, they found the strongest stylistic distinctions ran along the lines of **divine speech, human speech, and narration** — not J/E/P. [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/Genesis-Authorship-Computer-Assisted-Statistical-Linguistics/dp/8876531033) Critics note that stylistic unity could simply reflect composition in one epoch rather than one author. [Reformedperspectives](https://reformedperspectives.org/articles/bil_sichone/bil_sichone.DocumentaryHypothesis.pdf)
- **Koppel, Akiva, Dershowitz & Dershowitz (2011), "Unsupervised Decomposition of a Document into Authorial Components" (ACL P11-1136, Portland):** Using an unsupervised method (automatically generated Hebrew synonym sets clustered by an n-cut spectral method, then generalized with an SVM on function words), they split the Torah into two "families" that agreed with the consensus P/non-P scholarly division about 90% of the time. [Seforimblog](https://seforimblog.com/2011/07/attribution-and-misattribution-on/) Their control test demonstrated deconstruction of "an artificial book, Jer-iel, made up of randomly interleaved snippets from Jeremiah and Ezekiel," achieving ~98.8% accuracy. However, the number of clusters was an *input*, the split ran substantially along genre (legal vs. narrative) lines, Genesis 1 (near-universally called P) clustered with non-P, [Bible Interp](https://bibleinterp.arizona.edu/articles/der358009) and splitting into three-plus families did not reproduce the J/E/D divisions. [seforimblog](https://seforimblog.com/2011/07/attribution-and-misattribution-on/) Koppel explicitly rejected media claims that this proved multiple human authorship.
- **Yoffe et al. (2023), "A Statistical Exploration of Text Partition Into Constituents: The Priestly Source in Genesis and Exodus" (arXiv 2305.02170 / ACL Findings 2023):** Using tf-idf over lexemes and POS tags with k-means clustering and a conservative "cyclic label-shift" significance test, they reported statistically significant P/non-P overlap of about 90% in Exodus (p≈0.06) but only ~73% in Genesis (p≈0.08). [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2305.02170) The higher Exodus figure was driven largely by two big contiguous Tabernacle-construction blocks (Exod 25–31; 35–40), suggesting sensitivity to a semantic field rather than a pure stylistic fingerprint. (Scholarly consensus on P assignment itself runs ~96.5% in Genesis and ~97.3% in Exodus.)
- **Yoffe, Segev & Sober (2025), "An Information-Theoretic Approach to Identifying Formulaic Clusters" (arXiv 2503.07303):** An unsupervised entropy-based clustering method separated Genesis genealogies, Exodus P legal/cultic material, and (in Leviticus) the Priestly source from the Holiness Code [Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/computational-humanities-research/article/an-unsupervised-informationtheoretic-approach-to-identifying-formulaic-clusters-in-textual-data/46A801B34ECDC25D4B54BFCFF1FC1B8E) (H) with Matthews Correlation Coefficient scores commonly in the 85–95% range (up to 94% for Leviticus P vs. H, where the optimal window width of 28 verses matched the average length of priestly legislative units). They critique earlier supervised studies as relying on cherry-picked markers. [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.07303)

Distinctive P vocabulary markers repeatedly identified computationally include: numbers, "year" (shanah), "day" (yom), "generations"/toledot, "beget," "covenant" (berit), "circumcision," "create" (bara), "firmament," and the divine name "God" (Elohim); [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/html/2305.02170) non-P markers include dialogue verbs ("say/speak") and terms like "man" (ish).

**Isaiah.** Traditional attribution: the 8th-century prophet Isaiah of Jerusalem for all 66 chapters. Critical view (near-consensus for ~150 years): at least three hands — Proto-Isaiah (chs. 1–39, 8th c.), Deutero-Isaiah (chs. 40–55, an anonymous prophet of the Babylonian exile, ~540 BCE), and Trito-Isaiah (chs. 56–66, postexilic). The key triggers were historical (chs. 40–66 presuppose the exile already happened and name Cyrus of Persia in 44:28–45:1) [GotQuestions](https://www.gotquestions.org/Deutero-Isaiah.html) and stylistic/thematic.

Specific linguistic evidence cited:
- The verb *bara* ("to create") appears sixteen times in Isaiah 40–66 but only once in 1–39. [BYU Religious Studies Center](https://rsc.byu.edu/they-shall-grow-together/deutero-isaiah-book-mormon)
- Blenkinsopp's representative critical judgment: across the two major sections "the differences are more in evidence than the similarities." [The Interpreter Foundation](https://interpreterfoundation.org/journal/their-imperfect-best-isaianic-authorship-from-an-lds-perspective)
- **Radday, "Two Computerized Statistical-Linguistic Tests Concerning the Unity of Isaiah" (*Journal of Biblical Literature* 89.3, 1970; expanded 1973):** examined subconscious features (word length, syllable length, transitions). He found the second half has longer words — average syllable-length per word of 2.04 in chs. 1–35 vs. 2.11 in chs. 40–66 — and that inflected nouns increased by 63% in the second section, arguing for multiple authorship. [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/342564122_Isaiah's_Authorship_and_Methodology_A_Historical_Review) Asa Kasher's independent 1970 morphological study also concluded non-unity, though with different section divisions. [DOKUMEN.PUB](https://dokumen.pub/the-book-of-isaiah-chapters-139-0802823688.html) [BYU Religious Studies Center](https://rsc.byu.edu/isaiah-prophets/scientific-analysis-isaiah-authorship) Notably, Radday himself cautioned that style differences "even if objectively established, are consequently not author-specifying." [Scribd](https://www.scribd.com/document/486253397/The-Unity-and-Authorship-of-the-Book-of-pdf)
- Conservative counter-arguments (Gleason Archer, E. J. Young, Rachel Margalioth) stress the manuscript unity (the Great Isaiah Scroll from Qumran presents chs. 1–66 as a physical unit) and shared vocabulary/motifs, and argue the real driver of the split is a philosophical rejection of predictive prophecy.

**Psalms.** Traditional attribution: David ("the sweet psalmist of Israel"); the Talmud and some Dead Sea Scrolls ascribe the whole Psalter to David. [Hans Moscicke](https://hansmoscicke.wordpress.com/2017/11/15/intro-to-the-psalms-name-nature-canonicity-authorship-and-date/) Critical view: a multi-authored anthology composed over centuries. Only 73 psalms carry the superscription *le-David*, and the Hebrew preposition *le* can mean "to," "for," "belonging to," "about," or "according to" — not necessarily "by." [Hans Moscicke](https://hansmoscicke.wordpress.com/2017/11/15/intro-to-the-psalms-name-nature-canonicity-authorship-and-date/) Other superscriptions name the Sons of Korah (11×), Asaph (12×), Solomon (2×), and Heman, Ethan, and Moses (once each; Psalm 90 to Moses). [Hans Moscicke](https://hansmoscicke.wordpress.com/2017/11/15/intro-to-the-psalms-name-nature-canonicity-authorship-and-date/) Because psalms are short, liturgical, and formulaic, they are poor candidates for authorship stylometry; scholarly debate turns more on superscription meaning, historical anachronism (e.g., Psalm 137's exilic setting, Psalm 51's plea to rebuild Jerusalem's walls), [Hans Moscicke](https://hansmoscicke.wordpress.com/2017/11/15/intro-to-the-psalms-name-nature-canonicity-authorship-and-date/) and linguistic dating (Classical vs. Late Biblical Hebrew) than on computational style analysis.

**Historical and wisdom books.** Traditional attributions (Joshua for Joshua, Samuel for Samuel, Solomon for Proverbs/Ecclesiastes/Song of Songs, etc.) are widely treated by critical scholars as later or composite. Radday and colleagues applied statistical linguistics to Judges (1977) and Zechariah (1975), [Wiley Online Library](https://rss.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.2307/2982190) the latter examining the unity question for that prophetic book. The "Deuteronomistic History" (Joshua–Kings) is seen as an edited corpus. Ecclesiastes' Late Biblical Hebrew is widely taken as evidence against Solomonic authorship.

### New Testament

**The Gospels.** All four are formally anonymous — the "According to..." titles are later additions, and most scholars hold the traditions circulated anonymously before attribution in the 2nd century. Traditional attributions: Matthew (apostle), Mark (companion of Peter), Luke (companion of Paul), John (the beloved disciple). Critical dating: Mark first (~70 CE), then Matthew and Luke (~80–90), John last (~90–110). [arxiv](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1508.03772)

- **Synoptic problem:** The dominant solution is the Two-Source Hypothesis — Markan priority (Mark written first and used by Matthew and Luke) [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcan_priority) plus a hypothetical sayings source, Q (German *Quelle*), to explain the ~200 verses of "double tradition" shared by Matthew and Luke but absent from Mark. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-source_hypothesis) As Daniel B. Wallace summarizes, "Of Mark's 11,025 words, only 132 have no parallel in either Matthew or Luke. Percentage-wise, 97% of Mark's Gospel is duplicated in Matthew; and 88% is found in Luke." Rivals include the Farrer hypothesis (dispenses with Q; Luke used Matthew) and the Griesbach/Two-Gospel hypothesis (Matthew first). Q's existence has been increasingly questioned, notably by Mark Goodacre's *Case Against Q*. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Q_source)
- The heavy literary borrowing among the Synoptics means their similarities reflect *source dependence*, not common authorship; conversely John's distinct "Johannine" style sets it apart.
- A defense of traditional attribution (e.g., Catholic apologists, Bauckham on eyewitness tradition) stresses the *uniformity* of the manuscript attributions and the implausibility of independently inventing non-eyewitness names like Mark and Luke.

**Luke–Acts.** Traditional attribution: Luke the physician. Here stylometry supports *unity*: both are addressed to Theophilus, [Reasonable Theology](https://reasonabletheology.org/did-luke-write-the-gospel-of-luke/) and linguistic, structural, and stylistic parallels (shared vocabulary, mirrored prologues) lead most scholars to conclude a single author wrote both as a two-volume work. [Andrews University](https://digitalcommons.andrews.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=4223&context=auss) Several hundred words are unique to the Lukan corpus within the NT. [Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Authorship_of_Luke%E2%80%93Acts) A famous piece of evidence is the rare medical term *dysenterion* (dysentery) in Acts 28:8. [Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Authorship_of_Luke%E2%80%93Acts) A dissent by Albert C. Clark (1933) argued from particles and syntax that Luke and Acts were by different hands, but Wilfred L. Knox's rebuttal ("a man can be hanged for a fingerprint") is generally accepted. [Biblical Archaeology Society](https://library.biblicalarchaeology.org/article/who-wrote-the-gospel-of-luke/) The "we-passages" in Acts remain separately debated.

**Pauline corpus.** This is the most intensively studied case in all of stylometry.
- **Consensus groupings:** Seven *undisputed* letters (Romans, 1–2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, Philemon); six *disputed* (Ephesians, Colossians, 2 Thessalonians, 1–2 Timothy, Titus). [Patrick Milano](https://patrickmilano.com/2019/07/05/stylometry-and-the-pauline-epistles/) Hebrews is separately anonymous.
- **Pastorals (1–2 Timothy, Titus):** P. N. Harrison's *The Problem of the Pastoral Epistles* (1921) argued from hapax legomena (words appearing only once) that the Pastorals' vocabulary is markedly un-Pauline [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hapax_legomenon) . Per A. T. Robertson's summary of Harrison, "Words in the Pastorals, not elsewhere in the N.T. (Pastoral hapax legomena) 175 (168 according to Rutherford)"; over one-third of Pastoral vocabulary is absent from the other Pauline letters. Critics (W. P. Workman 1896; later Grayston & Herdan 1959; Michaelis) showed Harrison's per-page method was flawed and that variation among the *undisputed* letters is itself large. Workman's average hapax legomena per page of Greek text "ranged from 3.6 to 13," leading him to find the Pastorals' totals "not out of line with the others." Text length, topic, audience, and age all confound hapax counts. [HandWiki](https://handwiki.org/wiki/Hapax_legomenon)
- **Ephesians and Colossians:** Marked by long, complex sentences (Col 1:3–8 and 2:8–15 are each a single sentence in Greek), redundant piled-up phrases ("holy and blameless and irreproachable," Col 1:22), more participles and relative clauses, and fewer conjunctions than the undisputed letters. [Quizlet](https://quizlet.com/284187433/ephesians-and-colossians-study-notes-flash-cards/) Ephesians has about 72 hapax legomena. [Grokipedia](https://grokipedia.com/page/Epistle_to_the_Ephesians) Defenders note Colossians' hapax count is comparable to similar-length undisputed letters (Galatians has ~35) [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Colossians) and that heavy use of traditional/liturgical material could explain the un-Pauline feel. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Colossians)
- **2 Thessalonians:** Doubted since F. C. Baur on grounds of literary dependence on 1 Thessalonians, a more formal tone, and different eschatology (2 Thess 2 requires signs — the "man of lawlessness" — before the end, whereas 1 Thess 5 has the Lord come suddenly "like a thief"). Daryl Schmidt's syntactical comparison concluded 2 Thessalonians is highly unlikely to be Pauline. As Ernest Best noted, if 2 Thessalonians stood alone few would doubt it; the doubt arises only alongside 1 Thessalonians.

Computational Pauline studies — a century of conflicting results:
- **A. Q. Morton (1960s):** From sentence-length and function-word frequency (especially *kai*), argued only four letters (Romans, 1–2 Corinthians, Galatians) are genuinely Paul's. [Uliege](https://www.lasla.uliege.be/upload/docs/application/pdf/2022-05/45.pdf)
- **Anthony Kenny, *A Stylometric Study of the New Testament* (Oxford: Clarendon, 1986):** Using 99 linguistic features, ranked how "at home" each letter is in the corpus (Romans most, Titus least) and concluded, verbatim, "I see no reason to reject the hypothesis that twelve of the Pauline Epistles are the work of a single, unusually versatile author" — only Titus stood out as dubious.
- **David Mealand (1989, 1995/96):** Positional stylometry and multivariate analysis of 1000-word samples with ~19–25 stylistic variables. Found that Hebrews, 1 Peter, and 1 John cluster separately from Paul; within the Pauline corpus, the major and minor Paulines grouped together, but Colossians/Ephesians separated in one direction and the Pastorals in another. [Sage Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0142064X9601805904)
- **Jacques Savoy (2019), "Authorship of Pauline Epistles Revisited" (*JASIST*):** Using Burrows's Delta and Labbé's intertextual distance with hierarchical clustering, found four clusters — {Colossians, Ephesians}, {1–2 Thessalonians}, {Titus, 1–2 Timothy}, {Romans, Galatians, 1–2 Corinthians} — and an "impostors" verification indicating the {Colossians–Ephesians} pair was written by the same author who "seems not to be Paul." [Journal of the Association for Information Science and Technology](https://asistdl.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/asi.24176) (Critics such as Richard Carrier consider Savoy's methods result-driven.) [Richard Carrier Blogs](https://www.richardcarrier.info/archives/34609)
- **2025 reversal:** Pracht & McCauley, in two 2025 papers (including *Open Theology*), analyzed 18 "epistolary modes of discourse" using binomial/Poisson/negative-binomial models and found that, with few exceptions, the Pastorals do *not* differ from the authentic Paulines at a statistically significant level — contradicting much earlier work. An MDPI 2025 review ("Computational Stylometrics and the Pauline Corpus") argues that without a "secularly verifiable" authentic reference corpus, no reliable Pauline "stylome" can be defined, and that results are "characteristically incoherent and malleable." [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-1444/16/10/1264)

**Hebrews.** Traditionally grouped with Paul's letters, but internally anonymous. The Greek is more polished, the rhetoric more tightly constructed, and the vocabulary distinct from Paul. [Sharper Iron](https://sharperiron.org/article/authorship-of-hebrews-why-not-paul) Doubts are ancient: Clement of Alexandria suggested Paul wrote it in Hebrew and Luke translated it; Origen judged the thoughts Pauline but the composition another's, concluding "who wrote the epistle, in truth God knows." [Bible Doctrine](https://doctrine.org/who-wrote-hebrews) The author includes himself among second-generation Christians who received the gospel from eyewitnesses (Heb 2:3), contrary to Paul's insistence on direct revelation (Gal 1:12). [Bible Doctrine](https://doctrine.org/who-wrote-hebrews) Modern consensus: not by Paul. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorship_of_the_Epistle_to_the_Hebrews)

**Johannine literature.** Traditional attribution: the apostle John for the Gospel, three Epistles, and Revelation. The Gospel and 1 John share elegant, error-free Greek and common vocabulary/theology [Bart Ehrman Blog](https://ehrmanblog.org/who-wrote-the-book-of-revelation/) (Logos, light/darkness, witness/testimony). Revelation's Greek is rougher and heavily Semitic, with "barbarous idioms" and grammatical solecisms [Bible and Theology](https://www.bibleandtheology.net/authorship-of-the-book-of-revelation-part-2-what-about-the-differences-in-style/) — so distinct that Dionysius of Alexandria (mid-3rd c., preserved in Eusebius, *Ecclesiastical History* 7.25) concluded a different "John" wrote it, noting the author of Revelation names himself four times while the Gospel/Epistles author never does. [Prodigal Catholic](https://prodigalcatholic.com/the-author-of-the-book-of-revelation/) Modern scholars outside conservative circles commonly reject single authorship for the whole Johannine corpus; [Exegeticaltools](https://exegeticaltools.com/2020/05/24/who-is-the-john-of-revelation/) conservatives explain the differences by genre (apocalyptic) or the use of a secretary. [Gospel Mount](https://gospelmount.org/christian-living/who-wrote-the-book-of-revelation/)

**Catholic/General Epistles.**
- **1 and 2 Peter:** 1 Peter is in cultured, fluent Greek; 2 Peter is in a florid, "baroque," almost pretentious Greek with different favorite vocabulary. Richard Bauckham (*Jude, 2 Peter*, WBC) concludes: "The relationship of 1 and 2 Peter is ambiguous in its relevance to the question of Petrine authorship, but certainly Peter cannot be the real author of both letters" (he characterizes 2 Peter's Greek as "rather like baroque art, almost vulgar in its pretentiousness and effusiveness"). 2 Peter also overlaps heavily with Jude (scholarly consensus: 2 Peter used Jude) [Thirdmill](https://thirdmill.org/answers/answer.asp/file/41724) and had the weakest early attestation of any canonical book (Eusebius classed it among the *antilegomena*). [Garrett Ham](https://garrettham.com/second-peter/) Acts 4:13 describes Peter as *agrammatos* ("unlettered"). [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorship_of_the_Petrine_epistles) Defenders invoke different amanuenses — Silvanus named in 1 Peter 5:12 — to explain the divergence. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorship_of_the_Petrine_epistles)
- **James and Jude:** Attributed to Jesus' brothers; both disputed, partly on the quality of their Greek.

### Cross-cutting methodological cautions
- **Amanuenses:** Ancient secretaries were often given latitude to polish Greek, which can explain style shifts [Biblical Training](https://www.biblicaltraining.org/learn/academy/nt120-survey-of-the-new-testament/nt120-28-1-and-2-peter-jude) (e.g., 1 Peter via Silvanus, or a scribe for the Fourth Gospel) without changing the named author.
- **Genre and topic** strongly affect vocabulary: apocalyptic (Revelation), legal/cultic material (P), and pastoral church-order instructions (the Pastorals) naturally draw distinct word-fields.
- **Text length:** Most disputed letters are short (ten of the fourteen Pauline+Hebrews texts have under 5,000 words; Philemon has only ~388), making reliable attribution difficult; Philemon is often too short for secure classification. [Unine](http://members.unine.ch/jacques.savoy/Articles/StPaul.pdf)
- **Circularity:** Defining the "authentic" reference set in advance (e.g., assuming the seven-letter core) shapes the outcome; unsupervised methods that pre-judge nothing (e.g., White 2025) produce different groupings. [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-1444/16/10/1264)

## Recommendations

For a researcher or student building a reference on this topic:
1. **Treat stylometry as corroborative, not decisive.** Where stylometric, historical, and theological lines of evidence converge (Isaiah's multiple hands; Hebrews not Pauline; Revelation vs. the Fourth Gospel; 2 Peter vs. 1 Peter), conclusions are secure. Where only style is invoked and results conflict (the Pastorals, 2 Thessalonians), hold the conclusion loosely.
2. **Distinguish "consistent core" from "outliers."** The robust, replicated finding is the tight clustering of the seven undisputed Paulines and the Luke–Acts unity — use these as reference points. The disputed letters' status is the genuinely open question, and the 2025 studies show the trend is *away* from confident rejection.
3. **Weight recent unsupervised and information-theoretic methods** (Koppel 2011; Yoffe et al. 2023, 2025) for the OT and the newest discrete-distribution models (Pracht & McCauley 2025) for Paul, because they address the circularity and small-sample flaws of older per-page hapax counts and sentence-length tests.
4. **Always control for genre, topic, length, and amanuensis** before attributing a style difference to a different author.

**Benchmarks that would change these conclusions:** A large, independently-verified corpus of genuinely Pauline text (e.g., a newly authenticated letter) would create a real reference "stylome" and could settle the Pastorals question. Replicated unsupervised results that recover J/E/D (not just a two-way P/non-P or genre split) would strengthen the classical Documentary Hypothesis. Conversely, consistent findings that disputed letters fall within undisputed-corpus variation (as Pracht & McCauley and Van Nes suggest) would erode the stylometric case for pseudonymity.

## Caveats
- Many accessible summaries of these debates come from apologetic sites (defending traditional authorship) or skeptical blogs; I have prioritized peer-reviewed studies (JBL, NTS, JASIST, Open Theology, ACL proceedings) and named scholars, but some figures (e.g., Radday's "82%") originate in press reports of his work and should be verified against the primary monographs.
- "Scholarly consensus" is not unanimity: conservative and evangelical scholars continue to defend traditional single authorship for Isaiah, the Pastorals, 2 Peter, and Revelation, often on non-stylistic grounds (predictive prophecy, canonical status, amanuensis theory).
- Stylometry of ancient texts is constrained by textual transmission (later editorial punctuation affects sentence-length studies), translation issues, and the absence of large control corpora.
- The computational-source-critical results for the Pentateuch demonstrate statistically significant *separability* of P-like material but do not, by the authors' own admission, establish the number of human authors.
- Dates and some quantitative claims vary across sources; where I encountered conflicts (e.g., Harrison's vs. Rutherford's hapax counts, 175 vs. 168) I have flagged them.
